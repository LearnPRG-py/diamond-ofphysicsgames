Fluid Mechanics

Bramah's Press (Hydraulic Press)

Bramah's press or Hydraulic press uses the principle of pressure transmission and force multiplier in a fluid to generate higher output forces for a relatively small input force. This is used extensively in the industry in multiple equipment like cranes to lift heavy loads with a lower force, machines to crush metal etc.

The Principle

For an incompressible fluid, any applied pressure is transmitted equally throughout the fluid and in all directions.

Pressure: Pressure = Applied Force / Area of application

Pressure is measured in N/m2, also known as Pascals.

Incompressible fluids: Those where the volume and density do not change with change in pressure. Water is an incompressible fluid; oils are considered incompressible.

How Hydraulic Press Works

Consider a hydraulic system with 2 piston-cylinder arrangements which are connected and filled with an incompressible oil. Let the area of the smaller cylinder be A1 and that of the larger cylinder be A2.

On the smaller piston, let us apply a force F1. Let the output force on the larger piston be F2. Let us find the relation between F1 & F2.

The applied force of F1 on the small piston results in an applied pressure of P = F1 / A1.

As per the principle of equal transmission of pressure, P is transmitted equally throughout the fluid in this interconnected piston cylinder system. Thus, the larger piston is also subject to the same pressure P which results in a force of P × A2.

Force Multiplication in Hydraulic Press:

F2 = P × A2

P = F1 / A1

Therefore:

F2 = F1 × (A2 / A1)

Based on the relative sizes of A1 & A2, the force F2 gets multiplied several times over F1. This highlights the principle of the hydraulic press.

Example: Hydraulic Car Lift

A hydraulic car lift has a small piston with an area of 0.02 m2 and a large piston with an area of 0.5 m2. If a force of 100 N is applied to the small piston, what force is exerted by the large piston?

Given:

  • A1 = 0.02 m2
  • A2 = 0.5 m2
  • F1 = 100 N

Solution:

F2 = F1 × (A2 / A1)

F2 = 100 × (0.5 / 0.02)

F2 = 100 × 25

F2 = 2500 N

The large piston exerts a force of 2500 N, which is 25 times the input force!

Equation of Continuity

In fluids, mass flow rate is an important measure. A special class of fluids is incompressible fluids – one where the density does not change as pressure changes. Liquid (or fluid) like water is considered to be incompressible.

Equation of Continuity

In an incompressible fluid, the mass flow rate is same everywhere. This means when water (say) is flowing through a pipe whose diameter is changing, at any cross section of the pipe the same mass of water would flow past a probe every second.

Additionally, an incompressible fluid is one where the density does not change – this means given the mass flow rate is same and the density is constant, the volume flow rate will also be a constant at any cross section of the pipe.

Deriving the Equation

Consider 2 sections in the pipe at A and B. Let the radius at A be RA and at B be RB. Consider the velocity (speed) per second of the fluid (water) at A as vA and at B as vB. Let d be the constant density (which does not change since the fluid is incompressible).

The area of the pipe:

  • Area at A = π × RA × RA = π × RA2
  • Area at B = π × RB × RB = π × RB2

Volume and mass per second:

  • Volume through A per second = π × RA2 × vA
  • Mass through A per second = π × RA2 × vA × d
  • Volume through B per second = π × RB2 × vB
  • Mass through B per second = π × RB2 × vB × d

For an incompressible fluid (say water), the mass & volume flow rate at A & B will be equal. Hence:

π × RA2 × vA = π × RB2 × vB

or

vA × (RA)2 = vB × (RB)2

General Form (for any channel shape):

Velocity at A × Cross sectional Area at A = Velocity at B × Cross sectional Area at B

or

vA × AA = vB × AB

Key Insight: When the cross-sectional area of a pipe decreases, the velocity of the fluid increases, and vice versa. This is why water flows faster through narrow sections of a hose!

Example: Water Flow Through a Pipe

Water flows through a pipe with a cross-sectional area of 0.04 m2 at a velocity of 2 m/s. The pipe narrows to a cross-sectional area of 0.01 m2. What is the velocity of water in the narrow section?

Given:

  • A1 = 0.04 m2
  • v1 = 2 m/s
  • A2 = 0.01 m2
  • v2 = ?

Solution:

Using the equation of continuity:

v1 × A1 = v2 × A2

2 × 0.04 = v2 × 0.01

0.08 = v2 × 0.01

v2 = 0.08 / 0.01

v2 = 8 m/s

The velocity in the narrow section is 8 m/s, which is 4 times faster than in the wider section!
