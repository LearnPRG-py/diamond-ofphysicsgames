Energy and Force

What is Energy?

Energy is the ability to do work. There are multiple forms of energy, such as potential, kinetic, chemical, heat, etc. For example, a wrecking ball which has potential energy initially, gains kinetic energy and is able to do a lot of work.

Energy Conservation states that energy cannot be created or destroyed – it can only be transformed from one form to another.

The General theory of Relativity modified this concept to include Mass-Energy conservation which states that Mass and Energy together is conserved in a system – however Mass can be converted into Energy under special circumstances.

Mechanical Energy

While there are many forms of energy like Heat, Sound, Electrical etc., we will work here with Mechanical Energy. Mechanical energy is the energy a body possesses by virtue of its motion or position. It is of 2 types – Kinetic (KE) and Potential (PE).

In the presence of conservative forces (e.g. Non friction), the total mechanical energy of a body or system is conserved. That means the sum of the potential and kinetic energy is a constant – individually their value may change but the sum will always remain the same.

Potential Energy (PE)

One type of Potential energy that a body possess by virtue of its position with respect to other bodies is called Gravitational potential energy. It is directly proportional to the masses of the 2 bodies and inversely to the square of the distance between them.

Gravitational Potential Energy (near Earth's surface):

PE = m × g × h

Where:

  • m = mass of the body
  • g = gravitational acceleration (≈ 10 m/s2)
  • h = height from Earth's surface

The PE at the Earth's surface is taken to be 0. Please note that the PE is measured relative to a base where the PE is considered to be zero. This base can be chosen arbitrarily (as per one's wish). Thus potential energy can be negative too – for example the potential energy within Earth's surface is negative (if the Earth's surface is the base with PE = 0).

Kinetic Energy (KE)

The KE, i.e. the energy due to motion is measured as ½ × mass × (velocity)2. Here we use the magnitude of velocity and not its direction.

Kinetic Energy:

KE = ½ × m × v2

Both PE and KE are scalar quantities – they only have magnitude or value, no direction. Since KE is a scalar, sometimes people use speed & velocity interchangeably.

Since the total mechanical energy is unchanged in the absence of non-conservative forces, any increase or decrease in PE has a corresponding decrease or increase in KE.

Example: Ball Rolling Down a Hill

As the ball rolls down the top of a hill, the PE reduces (as height reduces) while the KE increases, which means the ball gains more speed/velocity. At the bottom of the hill (earth's surface, say), the PE is zero while the KE is at its maximum value.

Given:

  • Ball's mass = 1 kg
  • g = 10 m/s2
  • Height of hill = 100 m
  • Assuming absence of non-conservative forces

Find: Speed/velocity of the ball at the bottom of the hill

Solution:

PE at top = m × g × h = 1 × 10 × 100 = 1000 kg·m2/s2 = 1000 Joules

(kg·m2/s2 is called Joules, which is the SI unit of work, energy, etc.)

PE at bottom of hill (taken as base) = 0

Change (decrease) in PE = 1000 J which must be the change (increase) in KE

Initial KE = 0 (since the ball was at rest at the top of the hill)

Final KE = 0 + 1000 = 1000 J (since KE increased by 1000 J)

Let final velocity = v. Then:

½ × m × v2 = 1000

½ × 1 × v2 = 1000

v2 = 2000

v = √2000 = 44.7 m/s

The ball's velocity at the bottom is approximately 44.7 m/s

Force

Force is simply any push or pull. Forces, you could say are so much a part of physics, as letters are to English.

It has been said that Newton's first law gives a definition of Force while the second law gives a measurement of Force.

Newton's First Law

The first law defines force as something which changes the state of "rest" or "uniform motion" of a body. It states that a body will remain at rest or continue to move in a straight line at a constant speed, unless a resultant force acts on it.

Newton's Second Law

The second law measures force as mass × acceleration.

The second law states that the rate of change of momentum of a body is proportional to the applied force and it takes place in the direction in which the force acts.

Newton's Third Law

Newton's third law of motion states that when a body exerts a force on another body, the second body exerts a force of the same magnitude on the first body, but in the opposite direction.

Momentum

Momentum is defined as a product of a body's mass times its velocity:

Momentum:

p = m × v

Deriving F = m × a

Suppose a body of mass m is travelling at a velocity of u. Suppose a constant external force F acts on the body for time t. This force will change the velocity of the body; let the new velocity be v.

The change in momentum = m×v – m×u and this change happens in time t.

Thus, the rate of change of momentum = (m×v – m×u)/t = m×(v – u)/t

We know (v – u)/t is the change in velocity over time t which equals the acceleration (a) of the body.

From Newton's law we know rate of change of momentum is proportional to the external force.

Hence, F is proportional to m × a, i.e. F = k × m × a where k is a constant of proportionality.

We select k such that a force of 1 Newton on a mass of 1 kg produces an acceleration of 1 m/s2. Using the above units, we get k = 1 and thus the equation can be simplified as:

Force Equation:

F = m × a

a = F/m

The SI unit of Force is Newton, represented as N.

Example 1: Aircraft Take-off (Textbook Question 39)

A small aircraft of mass 520 kg needs to take-off with 30 m/s velocity using 200 m of runway.

1. Find the acceleration required

Given:

  • Initial velocity (u) = 0
  • Final velocity (v) = 30 m/s
  • Distance (s) = 200 m

Solution:

Using: v2 = u2 + 2as

302 = 02 + 2 × a × 200

900 = 400a

a = 900/400 = 2.25 m/s2

2. How much resultant force is needed for the take-off?

Solution:

Force needed = mass × acceleration

F = 520 × 2.25 = 1170 N

The aircraft needs 1170 N of force for take-off

Example 2: Force and Acceleration

A constant force of 10 N acting on a body of mass 2 kg will produce an acceleration of 5 m/s2.

Calculation: a = F/m = 10/2 = 5 m/s2

Example 3: Stopping a Moving Body

When a body of mass 5 kg travelling at 10 m/s is opposed by a force of 10 N acting constantly till it stops, the retardation (negative acceleration) is -2 m/s2. Note this is negative since the body's velocity is being reduced until it stops.

Calculation:

a = F/m = -10/5 = -2 m/s2

Time taken to stop:

Using: v = u + at

0 = 10 + (-2)t

t = 10/2 = 5 seconds

The body stops after 5 seconds
