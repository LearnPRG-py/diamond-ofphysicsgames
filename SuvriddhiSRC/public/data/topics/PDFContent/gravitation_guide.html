Gravitation

The Law of Universal Gravitation

The law of universal gravitation (propounded by Newton) says that a force of attraction exists between any 2 bodies. This is called the gravitational force. This force is proportional to the product of the masses of the 2 bodies and is inversely proportional to the distance between them (usually the distance between their centres of masses).

Thus for 2 masses M1 & M2 separated by r, the above law for the force F states:

F ∝ M1 × M2

and

F ∝ 1/r2

Therefore:

F = (G × M1 × M2) / r2

Where G is the universal gravitational constant

G = 6.67 × 10-11 N·m2/kg2

The value of G is the same throughout the universe.

Vector Form of Gravitational Force

The law can also be expressed in vector notations as:

F = G × M1 × M2 × r / r3

Where r shows the direction of the force

Note that the force is always attractive and acts along the line joining the 2 centres of masses.

Thus, the force F12 exerted by body 1 on body 2 is directed towards body 1's centre of mass and F21 exerted by body 2 on body 1 is directed towards body 2's centre of mass.

Newton's Third Law and Gravitation

As per Newton's 3rd law of motion, F12 is equal in magnitude but opposite in direction to F21.

Newton's Third Law Applied to Gravitation

F12 = -F21

Action & reaction are equal & opposite.

Systems with Multiple Bodies

When we have a system consisting of multiple bodies and want to analyse the effect of forces on a particular body of mass m due to all the other bodies of masses M1, M2, M3, ..., we should consider pairs of masses with one of them being m such as (m & M1), (m & M2), (m & M3) etc.

We compute the force on m due to each of the other masses in terms of magnitude and direction. A vector sum of all these individual forces will give the resultant force on m.

Applications of Gravitational Force

Several phenomena observed in nature are governed by gravitational forces of attraction & laws:

  • A body falling back to Earth when thrown up
  • The movement of planets around the Sun
  • The revolution of the Moon around the Earth
  • The motion of all heavenly bodies

Inverse Square Law: The law of universal gravitation follows the inverse square law (F is inversely proportional to r2)

Worked Example: Finding the Mass of Earth (Based on D1.2 from Textbook)

The Moon has an average distance of 384,000 km (3.84 × 108 m) from Earth, and goes around the Earth every 27.3 days (2,358,720 s).

Problem: Based on the information given, find the mass of the Earth (treat Earth and Moon as point masses)

Solution:

Take mass of Earth as M and of Moon as m.

We have that the gravitational force of attraction provides the centripetal force:

GmM / r2 = mv2 / r

Cancelling m and multiplying both sides by r:

GM / r = v2

The velocity of the Moon in its orbit is:

v = 2πr / t = (2π × 3.84 × 108) / 2,358,720

v ≈ 1022 m/s

Therefore:

GM / (3.84 × 108) = (1022)2 = 1,044,484

Solving for M:

M = (1,044,484 × 3.84 × 108) / G

M = (1,044,484 × 3.84 × 108) / (6.674 × 10-11)

M ≈ 6 × 1024 kg

The calculated mass of Earth is approximately 6 × 1024 kg

(The actual mass of Earth is 5.97 × 1024 kg)

Key Concepts Used:

  • Gravitational force provides the centripetal force for orbital motion
  • For circular motion: v = 2πr / T (where T is the orbital period)
  • The universal gravitational constant G is known
  • We can determine the mass of celestial bodies using orbital mechanics
