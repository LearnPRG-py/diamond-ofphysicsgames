Sound Waves

What is Sound?

We experience sound all throughout our daily lives, from the cars on the road, to music.

Sound is a longitudinal wave. This means that the direction of propagation of the wave and oscillation of particles are parallel. A sound wave also travels equally in all directions.

Sound Wave Components

A sound wave has both a displacement wave and a pressure wave which are phase shifted by π/2 radians (90°). This means when one is at its minimum or maximum, the other is at 0.

Wave Velocity

For any wave, including sound waves, the velocity of the wave is the product of its frequency and wavelength.

Wave Velocity Equation:

v = λ × f

Where:

  • v = velocity of the wave
  • λ = wavelength
  • f = frequency

Speed of Sound

Sound waves require a medium to travel through. The speed of sound in air is a constant 340 m/s, or 1224 km/hr.

For perspective:

  • About 1.5 times the cruising speed of an average airplane
  • About 3.5 times the speed of an F1 car

Standing Waves in Tubes

When there is a tube:

  • An open end corresponds to a displacement antinode, but a pressure node
  • A closed end corresponds to a pressure antinode, but a displacement node

Node: A point where the pressure or displacement can't change

Antinode: A point where pressure or displacement can reach their maximum or minimum in a standing wave

As the name suggests, standing waves are stationary. This means that they do not propagate.

Types of Tubes

Usually, we have tubes with:

  • a. Both ends open
  • b. Only one end open

Important: The distance between 2 consecutive nodes or antinodes is half the wavelength (λ/2).

Tube with Both Ends Open

In a tube with both ends open, the length of the tube is equal to the harmonic number, multiplied by half the wavelength.

Tube with Both Ends Open:

L = n × λ/2

Where:

  • L = length of tube
  • n = harmonic number (1, 2, 3, ...)
  • λ = wavelength

Combining with v = λf:

L = n × v / (2f)

This allows us to find L based on frequency and wave velocity instead of wavelength.

Tube with One End Open

For a tube with one end open, we use a different formula since it has a node at one end and an antinode on the other in the fundamental mode, making the distance ½ of λ/2 or λ/4.

Tube with One End Open:

L = n × λ/2 - λ/4

or

L = (2n - 1) × λ/4

Where:

  • L = length of tube
  • n = harmonic number (1, 2, 3, ...)
  • λ = wavelength

Combining with v = λf:

L = (2n - 1) × v / (4f)

Musical Applications

Standing waves have various musical applications. Instruments such as flutes use tubes open at both ends.

Example: Flute Tube Length for Note A4

The note A4 is calibrated at 440 Hz. You are in a room with the speed of sound as 340 m/s. How long does the tube need to be to play that note in the fundamental mode?

Given:

  • Frequency (f) = 440 Hz
  • Speed of sound (v) = 340 m/s
  • Harmonic number (n) = 1 (fundamental mode)
  • Tube open at both ends

Solution:

Step 1: Find the wavelength

λ = v / f = 340 / 440 = 0.772 m

Step 2: Find the tube length

For fundamental mode (n = 1) in a tube open at both ends:

L = n × λ/2

L = 1 × 0.772/2

L = 0.386 m = 38.6 cm

The tube needs to be 38.6 cm long to play the note A4 in the fundamental mode.

Key Takeaways:

  • Sound is a longitudinal wave that requires a medium
  • The speed of sound in air is 340 m/s
  • Standing waves in tubes create musical notes
  • Different tube configurations (open/closed ends) produce different harmonic patterns
  • The fundamental mode is the lowest frequency (n = 1)
