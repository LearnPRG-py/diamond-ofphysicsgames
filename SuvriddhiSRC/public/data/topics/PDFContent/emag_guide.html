Electrical Forces - Coulomb's Law

When we have 2 charged particles, each will exert a force on the other. This force is defined & governed by Coulomb's law.

Coulomb's Law

The force between 2 charged bodies is directly proportional to the product of the magnitude of the 2 charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.

This force is attractive if the charges are of opposite signs and repulsive if they have the same sign.

Note: The similarity with the law of gravitation, except that in the case of gravitational force, it is always attractive.

Coulomb's Law (Mathematical Form):

F = k × q1 × q2 / r2

Where:

  • q1 and q2 = the 2 charges
  • r = distance separating them (measured along the line joining the charges)
  • k = Coulomb's constant

Vector Form of Coulomb's Law

The law can also be expressed in vector notations as:

F = k × q1 × q2 × r / r3

The direction of the force is given by r and for a 2 charge system, it is along the line joining the 2 point charges or centre of the 2 charges.

For two point charges 1 & 2, the force exerted by charge 1 on 2 is F12 and that by charge 2 on 1 is represented by F21. As per Newton's 3rd law of motion which states action & reaction are equal in magnitude but opposite in direction:

F12 = -F21

Coulomb's Constant and Permittivity

The constant k is also written as:

k = 1 / (4πε0)

Where ε0 is called the permittivity of free space

ε0 = 8.85 × 10-12 C2 N-1 m-2

Systems with Multiple Charges

When we have a system of charges and want to analyse the effect of forces on a particular charge (q) due to all the other charges (q1, q2, q3, ...), consider pairs of charges with one of them being q such as (q & q1), (q & q2), (q & q3) etc.

We compute the force on q due to each of the other charges in terms of magnitude and direction. A vector sum of all these individual forces will give the resultant force on q.

Example: Force Between Two Point Charges

2 point charges have charges of 4.5 × 10-8 C and -1.3 × 10-7 C, separated by 3.2 cm (0.032 m). What is the magnitude of the force of attraction between them?

Given:

  • q1 = 4.5 × 10-8 C
  • q2 = -1.3 × 10-7 C
  • r = 0.032 m
  • k = 9 × 109 N·m2/C2

Solution:

The force of attraction between them:

F = k × q1 × q2 / r2

F = (9 × 109 × 4.5 × 10-8 × -1.3 × 10-7) / (0.032)2

F = -0.0514 N

The magnitude of the force is 0.0514 N, and the force is attractive, as represented by the negative sign.

Resistance in Circuits

An important concept in circuits is resistance. Resistance can help in reducing current going through components, in turn protecting them. This concept, with numerous applications has its own physics.

Ohm's Law

Ohm's Law

Under constant temperature and pressure conditions, the Voltage or potential difference equals Current times Resistance.

Ohm's Law:

V = I × R

Where:

  • V = Voltage (potential difference)
  • I = Current
  • R = Resistance

Resistors in Series

If there are two resistors in series (one after another), let us say of resistance R1 and R2, then the resistance is R1 + R2.

Series Circuit Characteristics:

  • The current throughout the path is the same
  • The potential difference (PD) across the resistors is different

Resistors in Series:

Rtotal = R1 + R2 + R3 + ...

Resistors in Parallel

If there are two resistors in parallel (in separate branches), assuming resistance of R1 and R2, the net resistance R can be found using:

Resistors in Parallel:

1/R = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + 1/R3 + ...

Parallel Circuit Characteristics:

  • The current through each branch is different
  • The potential difference (PD) across the resistors is the same
  • More resistance will mean less current through the branch
  • The net resistance (R) will be less than both R1 and R2

Example: Current Distribution in Parallel Circuit

A circuit with resistors of 5Ω and 1Ω in parallel will mean that most current will go through the branch with the 1Ω resistor.

Explanation: Current takes the path of least resistance. Since the 1Ω resistor offers less resistance than the 5Ω resistor, more current flows through that branch.

Important: In a parallel circuit, the net resistance (R) will always be less than the smallest individual resistance in the circuit.
